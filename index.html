01
Research Agenda

A Unified Theory of Curriculum Structure

Three projects. One mission-critical bottleneck. The absence of a shared formal language for what curricula actually teach.

G

The Language Problem

Instructors and departments make consequential structural decisions without a shared vocabulary precise enough to reason about. Every intervention study starts from scratch. Every finding is trapped in one institution's context. The bottleneck is not data — it is the absence of a formal language.

D

The Cost Problem

Every instructional shortcut — introducing notation before intuition, coverage before comprehension — imposes a cost on student understanding that is currently invisible. Software engineering named this phenomenon two decades ago. Education has not.

*

The Sufficiency Problem

We debate curricula in terms of coverage. No one asks: of everything in this curriculum, what is actually necessary to produce the stated competency? This is a well-defined optimization problem. Its NP-hardness has now been proved. Its implications for equity are profound.

Paper 01
Formal Methods · Learning Analytics Target: EDM 2026 · LAK 2026

Toward a Typed Dependency Grammar for Undergraduate Curricula

A formal language for describing what curricula actually teach

We introduce the Typed Dependency Grammar (TDG) — a directed graph in which learning objectives are nodes and typed edges encode four classes of dependency: conceptual, procedural, motivational, and social. We prove well-formedness is decidable in polynomial time and provide preliminary empirical evidence that structural violations predict student attrition.

Paper 02
NLP · Static Analysis · Pedagogy Target: ACM Learning @ Scale · ICER 2026

Pedagogical Debt: Formalizing and Auditing Accumulated Instructional Shortcuts

A static analysis tool for course material quality

We introduce pedagogical debt — the accumulated cost to student understanding of instructional shortcuts — grounded in Harel's necessity principle and Jeff Anderson's twelve modeling criteria. We build PedDebt, an open-source static analysis tool validated at Cohen's κ = 0.71 against expert instructors.

Three Debt Types

Paper 03
Algorithms · Complexity Theory · Curriculum Design Target: ICER 2026 · NeurIPS Education Workshop

The Minimum Viable Curriculum Problem: Complexity, Structure, and Instructional Sufficiency

An NP-hardness proof and tractable special cases for optimal curriculum design

We introduce and formalize the MVC problem, prove its NP-hardness via reduction from Set Cover, identify polynomial special cases, provide a greedy approximation algorithm, and design a hands-on student activity meeting all twelve of Jeff Anderson's modeling criteria.

Key Results

  • Greedy ln|T|-approximation algorithm

Student Activity

Where This Research Comes From

The Institution Seen from Every Angle

"The research isn't despite the student services work. The research is the student services work, made formal."

Most CS education researchers arrive at questions about student success from the outside — from data, from literature, from the lab. I arrived at them from inside the institution, from five different roles in which I watched the same students encounter the same invisible structural barriers from completely different vantage points.

In financial aid, I saw students leave STEM because a funding gap at the wrong moment made a hard class feel unsurvivable. In counseling, I heard students describe themselves as "not a math person" — a belief constructed from curricula that never explained why anything mattered. In the Teaching and Learning Center, I watched students spend hours on content their course objectives barely required. In learning communities and affinity groups, I saw students discover for the first time that their peers were struggling too — and that this shared knowledge was what let them stay.

The research questions in these three papers are not abstractions. They are formalized versions of things I watched happen to real students. I do not come to this research with the most advanced CS portfolio in my cohort. What I bring is an unusually complete picture of how students actually move through institutions — and an unusually strong conviction that the problems are structural, solvable, and worth a career.

Financial Aid

Watched students withdraw from STEM not from lack of ability but because financial precarity made any obstacle feel final. Learned that structural interventions outperform individual counseling every time.

→ Grounds the equity focus of all three papers

Counseling Support

Heard students articulate fixed beliefs about their own mathematical ability — beliefs formed from curricula that never established why the material mattered. Introduced to Harel's necessity principle not from theory but from watching what happened when it was violated.

→ Motivational debt · Necessity principle

Teaching & Learning Center

Tutored students navigating content their course objectives barely required. Began asking: how much of what students are struggling with is actually necessary for where they are trying to go? This question became the Minimum Viable Curriculum problem.

→ Direct origin of Paper 03

Learning Communities

Saw peer context transform students' relationship to difficulty. A student who believes they are uniquely confused is fragile. One who learns confusion is shared and structural is resilient. Learning communities make the structural visible — exactly what this research does computationally.

→ Social dependency type in TDG

Affinity Groups

Worked with students told in both explicit and subtle ways that STEM was not for them. Saw how belonging-based interventions changed persistence more reliably than content-based ones. Began asking: what would curriculum look like that creates belonging as a structural feature, not an add-on?

→ Equity framing · MVC-distance and disproportionate burden

"The goal is not a better prediction model. The goal is curriculum that builds students' belief in themselves, their tolerance for hard problems, and their capacity for deep learning — by design, not by accident."

This research is a first step toward translating a decade of watching students into tools that instructors and institutions can actually use. The papers are rigorous because the problems deserve rigor. The tools are open because the students who need them most are already paying too much.

The long-term vision is project-based curriculum grounded in this research — learning experiences that activate intrinsic motivation, reward intellectual courage, and make students the authors of their own understanding.

About
the
Researcher

I am a first-generation college student, McNair Scholar at San Jose State University, and Research Lead at the Foothill College Science Learning Institute. Before this research, I spent years working in student services — financial aid, counseling, tutoring, learning communities, affinity groups — watching the same students encounter the same invisible structural barriers from every angle the institution offers.

That experience is the origin of this work, not a detour from it. The questions in these papers are formalized versions of things I watched happen to real students. Pedagogical debt is the cost I watched them pay. MVC-distance is the invisible weight I watched them carry.

The long-term goal is to turn this research into project-based curriculum that builds students' belief in themselves, their tolerance for hard problems, and their capacity for deep learning — by activating intrinsic motivation and by design, not by accident.

This work is dedicated to Jeff Anderson, whose Applied Linear Algebra Fundamentals textbook and twelve criteria for modeling activities are the existence proof that the problem is solvable — and that solving it is worth a career.
